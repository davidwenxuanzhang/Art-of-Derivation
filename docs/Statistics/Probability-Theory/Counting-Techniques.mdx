## Fundamental Principle of Counting

:::tip Fundamental Principle of Counting
Suppose $n$ decisions are made in sequence, where at the $i$th step there are $k_i$ available options. If each decision is made **independently** (meaning the outcome of one will not influence or be influenced by any other), then the total number of unique ways to perform all $n$ decisions is

$$
\prod_{i=1}^{n}k_{i}=k_1\times k_2\times \cdots \times k_n
$$
:::

## Ways of Counting

There are four counting methods, depending on two questions: does order matter? And can choices repeat? 

When order matters, we call it **Permutations**; on the contrary, when order does not matters, we call it **Combinations**.

When choices can repeat, we say the selection is **with Replacement**; on the contrary, when choices cannot repeat, we call it **without Replacement**.

### Permutations with Replacement

:::tip Permutations with replacement 

If there are $n$ unique items, then the number of *Permutations* to select $k$ items with replacement is:

$$
n^k 
$$

<details>
<summary><span className="math-keyword">Derivation</span></summary>

By the Fundamental Principle of Counting, this is just $k$ independent steps, where at each step there are $n$ choices avaliable (since replacement means there is nothing moved from the pool). Therefore, the number of Permutations is :

$$
\underbrace{n\times n\times \cdots \times n}_{k\text{times}}=n^k \tag*{$\blacksquare$}
$$

</details>
::: 

### Permutations without Replacement

:::tip Permutations without Replacement
If there are $n$ unique items, then the number of *Permutations* to select $k$ items without replacement is:

$$
\frac{n!}{(n-k)!}
$$
<details>
<summary><span className="math-keyword">Derivation</span></summary>

By the Fundamental Principle of Counting, this is just $k$ independent steps, but since items are not replaced, the pool shrinks by one at each step: $n$ choice in step 1, $n-1$ choice in step two, and so on down to $n-k+1$ in step $k$. Therefore, the number of Permutations is :

$$
n\times (n-1)\times (n-2)\times \cdots\times (n-k+1) =\frac{n!}{(n-k)!} \tag*{$\blacksquare$}
$$

</details>
:::

### Combinations without Replacement

:::tip Combinations without Replacement
If there are $n$ unique items, then the number of *Combinations* to select $k$ items without replacement is denoted $\binom{n}{k}$, read as "**n choose k**", and defined as:

$$
\binom{n}{k}\overset{\text{def}}{=}\frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}
$$
This could also be found in [Binomial Theorem]

<details>
<summary><span className="math-keyword">Derivation</span></summary>

Start with permutation without Replacement, and the number of *Permutations* to select $k$ items from $n$ without replacement is:

$$
\frac{n!}{(n-k)!}
$$

But since order doesn't matter, every group of $k$ items gets counted $k!$ times, so we divide out the overcounting and get:

$$
\frac{n!}{(n-k)!}\div {k!}=\frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}=\binom{n}{k} \tag*{$\blacksquare$}
$$

</details>
:::

### Combinations with Replacement

::::tip Combinations with Replacement
If there are $n$ unique items, then the number of *Combinations* to select $k$ items with replacement is:

$$
\binom{n+k-1}{n-1}=\binom{n+k-1}{k}
$$


<details>
<summary><span className="math-keyword">Derivation</span></summary>

This scenario is a little tricky, so we will follow an example: 

:::warning Pirates split coins

3 pirates are picking 10 identical coins, where each pirate can receive zero or more coins. How many ways can this be done?

:::

:::info Solution
This is a Combinations with Replacement problem because the coins are identical so order doesn't matter (*Combination*), and each pirate can receive more than one coin (*with Replacement*). Instead of directly counting distributions among pirates, we can transform 3 pirates into 2 partitions that split the coins, shown as:

$$
\star\star\star \underbrace{|}_{\text{partition}} \star\star\star\star \star \underbrace{|}_{\text{partition}} \star\star
$$

Then, the problem just became Combinations without Replacement that choose 2 positions for partitions among all 12 positions (or choose 10 positions for coins among all 12 positions), which is:
$$
\binom{12}{2}=\binom{12}{10}=66
$$
Correspondingly, when $n$ pirates pick $k$ identical coins, the number of *Combinations* with replacement is: 

$$
\binom{n+k-1}{n-1}=\binom{n+k-1}{k} \tag*{$\blacksquare$}
$$
:::

</details>

::::

### Summary 

<div style={{display: 'flex', justifyContent: 'center'}}>

|:---:|:---:|:---:|

| **Without Replacement** | $\dfrac{n!}{(n-k)!}$ | $\dbinom{n}{k} = \dfrac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$ |
