### De Morgan's Law

:::tip De Morgan's Law 
For two events $A$ and $B$, then
$$
(A\cup B)^{c}=A^{c}\cap B^{c}
$$
$$
(A\cap B)^{c}=A^{c}\cup B^{c}
$$

### Complement Rule {#Complement-Rule}

:::tip Complement Rule

For any event $A$:
$$
P(A^{c})=1-P(A)
$$
<details>
<summary><span className="math-keyword">Derivation</span></summary>

From the definition of Complement, we know that $A$ and $A^{c}$ are mutually exclusive, and they form the entire sample space, as:
$$
A\cup A^{c}=\Omega
$$
By [Countable Additivity](docs\Statistics\Probability-Theory\Probability-Basics.mdx#Probability-Axioms):
$$
P(A\cup A^{c})=P(A)+P(A^{c})=P(\Omega) \overset{\text{Normalization}}{=} 1
$$
Hence:
$$
P(A^{c})=1-P(A)  \tag*{$\blacksquare$}
$$
</details>
:::


### Probability of the Empty Set
:::tip Probability of the Empty Set

The empty event has probability zero, as:

$$
P(\varnothing)=0
$$
<details>
<summary><span className="math-keyword">Derivation</span></summary>

Aa $\Omega$ represent all possible outcome, the complement of it according to definition is $\varnothing$, as:

$$
\Omega^{c}=\varnothing
$$

By [Complement Rule](#Complement-Rule)：
$$
P(\varnothing)=1-P(\Omega)\overset{\text{Normalization}}{=}1-1=0 \tag*{$\blacksquare$}
$$

</details>
:::

### Probability Bounds

:::tip Probability Bounds 

For any event $A$, probability is between 0 and 1, as:

$$
0\le P(A) \le 1
$$

<details>
<summary><span className="math-keyword">Derivation</span></summary>

By the [Non-negativity Axiom](docs\Statistics\Probability-Theory\Probability-Basics.mdx#Probability-Axioms):

$$
0\le P(A)
$$
Also, by the [Complement Rule](#Complement-Rule):

$$
P(A^{c})=1-P(A)
$$

Since probabilities are non-negative:
$$
P(A^{c})\ge 0
$$
Therefore: 
$$
1-P(A)\ge 0 \implies P(A) \le 1
$$

Hence: 
$$
0\le P(A) \le 1  \tag*{$\blacksquare$}
$$
</details>
:::

### Subset Rule

:::tip Subset Rule

For any event that $A$ $\subseteq$ $B$: 

$$
P(A)\le P(B)
$$
<details>
<summary><span className="math-keyword">Derivation</span></summary>

If $A$ $\subseteq$ $B$, then event $B$ could split into two mutually exclusive parts, one with event $A$ and one without, as:

$$
B=A\cup (B\cap A^{c} )
$$
where

$$
A\cap (B\cap A^{c} )=\varnothing
$$

By [Countable Additivity](docs\Statistics\Probability-Theory\Probability-Basics.mdx#Probability-Axioms):
$$
P(B)=P(A\cup (B\cap A^{c}) )=P(A)+P(B\cap A^{c} )
$$

Also, by the [Non-negativity Axiom](docs\Statistics\Probability-Theory\Probability-Basics.mdx#Probability-Axioms):

$$
P(B\cap A^{c} ) \ge 0
$$

Therefore: 
$$
P(B)=P(A)+P(B\cap A^{c}) \ge P(A)
$$

Hence:
$$
P(A)\le P(B) \tag*{$\blacksquare$}
$$
</details>
:::

### Addition Rule

:::tip Addition Rule

For any event $A$ and $B$:

$$
P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B)
$$

<details>
<summary><span className="math-keyword">Derivation</span></summary>

For the event $A\cup B$, it could split into two mutually exclusive parts, one with event $A$ and one with event $B$ but not with $A$, as: 

$$
A\cup B= A\cup (B\cap A^{c})
$$

where

$$
A\cap (B\cap A^{c} )=\varnothing
$$

By [Countable Additivity](docs\Statistics\Probability-Theory\Probability-Basics.mdx#Probability-Axioms):
$$
P(A\cup B)= P(A)+P(B\cap A^{c}) \tag ①
$$

For the event $B$, it could also split into two mutually exclusive parts, one with event $A$ and one without, as: 
$$
B=(A\cap B)\cup ( B\cap  A^{c})
$$

And because these two parts are also mutually exclusive, again, by [Countable Additivity](docs\Statistics\Probability-Theory\Probability-Basics.mdx#Probability-Axioms):
$$
P(B)=P(A\cap B)+P( B\cap  A^{c})
$$
$$
P( B\cap  A^{c})=P(B)-P(A\cap B)
$$

Plug in back to ①:

$$
A\cup B= P(A)+P(B\cap A^{c})=  P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B)  \tag*{$\blacksquare$}
$$
</details>

:::
