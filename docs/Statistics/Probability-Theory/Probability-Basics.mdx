## Classical Definition of Probability

:::tip Sample Space

The **sample space** is the set of all possible outcomes of an experiment, denoted by $\Omega$.

:::

:::tip Event

An **event** $A$ is the subset of sample space $\Omega$, denoted as $A\subseteq \Omega$.

:::

::::tip Classical Definition of Probability

Assume the sample space $\Omega$ consists all equally likely outcomes, then the probability of an event $A$ is: 

$$
P(A)\overset{\text{def}}{=}\frac{\text{numbers of outcomes in }A}{\text{numbers of possible outcomes in }\Omega}=\frac{|A|}{|\Omega|}
$$
:::danger WARNING
The classical definition of probability only applies when the sample space is **finite** and all outcomes are **equally likely**.
:::

::::

To use the classical definition of probability, we usually follow three steps: first identify the sample space $\Omega$, then describe the event $A$, and finally count the number of outcomes in each set.

The following example shows how these definitions work together.

A fair six-sided die is rolled once; what is the probability of rolling an even number?

:::info Solution

Since it is a six-sided die, the sample sapce is: 

$$
\Omega=\{1,2,3,4,5,6\}
$$

Let $A$ be the event of rolling an even number:

$$
A=\{2,4,6\}
$$

Then:
$$
|A|=3, \qquad |\Omega|=6
$$

Since all outcomes are equally likely, by the classical definition of probability:

$$
P(A)=\frac{|A|}{|\Omega|}=\frac{3}{6}=\frac{1}{2}
$$

## Axiomatic Definition of Probability

As we warned before, the classical definition of probability only applies when the sample space is **finite** and all outcomes are **equally likely**, and here is a counter example.
::::warning Circle in a Square
<div className="deriv2col" style={{ gridTemplateColumns: "270px 1fr"}}>

  <div>
    <img src={useBaseUrl('/img/Square-Circle.png')} width="260" style={{ transform: "translateY(100px)" }}/>
  </div>
 
  <div>
A circle is inscribed in a square with side length 1. If a point is chosen uniformly at random inside the square, what is the probability that the point lies inside the circle?
:::danger False Attempt
When attempt the classical definition of probability:
$$
P(A)=\frac{|A|}{|\Omega|}
$$
where $A$ is the set of points inside the circle and $\Omega$ is the set of all points inside the square.

However, both $A$ and $\Omega$ contain infinitely many points, so we cannot compute the probability by counting outcomes, as: 

$$
P(A)=\frac{|A|}{|\Omega|}=\frac{\infty}{\infty}={❌}
$$
:::
  </div>
</div>

::::

This example shows why probability needs a more general definition. Instead of counting equally likely outcomes, we want probability to assign a consistent “size” to events, whether those events are finite sets or infinite sets.

### Set Theory for Probability

#### Sample Space and Event

:::tip Events as Sets

In probability, an event is a set of outcomes. If $\Omega$ is the sample sapce, then every event $A$ satisfy:

$$
A \subseteq \Omega
$$

Hence, instead of only counting individual outcomes, we can study how events relate to each other as sets.
:::

#### Union

:::tip Union

The **union** of two events $A$ and $B$, denoted by $A\cup B$, is the event either $A$ or $B$ happens，also refered to "$A$ or $B$".

#### Intersection

:::tip Intersection
The **intersection** of two events $A$ and $B$, denoted by $A\cap B$, is the event both $A$ and $B$ happens, also refered to "$A$ and $B$".

#### Complement

:::tip Complement
The **complement** of an event $A$, denoted by $A^{c}$, is the event that $A$ does not happen，also refered to "not $A$".

:::tip Disjoint/Mutually Exclusive
We call a collection of events as **disjoint** when their intersection is empty. It is also refered as **mutually exclusive** because they cannot happen simultaneously.

:::tip Axiomatic Definition of Probability

According to Kolmogorov’s axioms, a probability function $P$ assigns a number to each event and satisfies three basic rules:

1. **Non-negativity**: For every event $A$, probabilities are never negative
$$
P(A)\geq 0
$$

2. **Normalization**: The probability of the entire sample space is one
$$
P(\Omega)=1
$$

3. **Countable Additivity**: If events $A_1,A_2,A_3,\dots$ are pairwise disjoint (or mutually exclusive), then

$$
P\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} A_i\right)=\sum_{i=1}^{\infty}P(A_i)
$$

:::
