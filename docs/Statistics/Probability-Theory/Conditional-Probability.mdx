## Definition of Conditional Probability {#conditional-probability}

:::tip Conditional Probability

For two events $A$ and $B$ with $P(B)>0$, the **Conditional Probabilty** of event $A$ happens given that event $B$ has happened is defined as:

$$
P(A\mid B)\overset{\text{def}}{=}\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)}
$$

:::

## Multiplication Rule

:::tip Multiplication Rule for Two Event

If $A$ and $B$ are two events with $P(A)>0$ and $P(B)>0$, then the multiplcation rule says: 

$$
\begin{align*}
P(A\cap B)&=P(A) P(B\mid A)\\
          &= P(B) P(A\mid B)
\end{align*}
$$

<details>
<summary><span className="math-keyword">Derivation</span></summary>

According to the definition of [conditional probability](docs\Statistics\Probability-Theory\Conditional-Probability.mdx#conditional-probability):

$$
P(A\mid B)\overset{\text{def}}{=}\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)}
$$

Multiplying both sides by $P(B)$ we get:

$$
P(A\cap B)=P(A) P(B\mid A)
$$

Similarly from $P(B\mid A)$ we may also get: 

$$
P(A\cap B)=P(B) P(A\mid B)
$$

Hence: 

$$
\begin{align*}
P(A\cap B)&=P(A) P(B\mid A)\\
          &= P(B) P(A\mid B)   \tag*{$\blacksquare$}
\end{align*}
$$
</details>
:::

We can generalize this multiplcation rule to more events; first, we can simplify the notation of intersection as :

$$
P(A_1\cap A_2 \cap \cdots \cap A_n)\overset{\text{def}}{=}P(A_1,A_2,\cdots, A_n)
$$

where comma inside could read as "and", as:

- $P(A_1,A_2,\cdots, A_n)$ read as "the probability of events $A_1$ and $A_2$ and ... and $A_n$ all happen."
- $P(A\mid B_1, B_2, \cdots , B_n)$ read as "the conditional probability of event $A$ happens given events $B_1$ and $B_2$ and ... and $B_n$ all have happened."

:::tip Multiplication Rule

If events $A_1,A_2,\cdots , A_n$ all have positive probability, then:

$$
P(A_1, A_2, \cdots, A_n)=P(A_1) P(A_2\mid a_1) P(A_3\mid A_1, A_2)\cdots P(A_n\mid A_1,\cdots, A_{n-1})
$$

</details>

:::

## Conditional Probability as Probability Function

:::tip Conditional Probability as Probability Function

Conditional Probability could be considered as a probability function as it fits all three [probability axioms](docs\Statistics\Probability-Theory\Probability-Basics.mdx#Probability-Axioms), and we define it as:
$$
\widetilde{P}(A)\overset{\text{def}}{=} P(A \mid B).
$$
